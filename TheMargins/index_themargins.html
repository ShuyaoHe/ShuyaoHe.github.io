IT TAKES TWO TO TRANSITION

The Story Behind Transgender Teens and Their Parents

By Rachel Grozanick, Andy Robinson, Sophia Fox-Sowell, Shuyao He and Lena Young

Video | Kimma transitioned from male to female in 20XX at age X. She was originally named after her father, as Craig Jr.







“I would do anything to protect my son, including murder somebody. But now it’s my son that wants to take him away. So, do I kill you or do I love you? I chose the latter, but it certainly wasn’t natural or naturally easy. And it still isn’t.”





Craig Hall looks intently at his eldest daughter as she sits in front of her computer wearing a pink sweatshirt. She shows us her artwork, a colorful self portrait of a budding teenager with long brown hair — a reflection she only began seeing in the mirror about a year ago.

At 13 years old, Kimma is already more self-aware than her classmates. Eager to stay informed about the world, she actively watches the news, a program her friends find “sad and depressing.” She worries about who her generation will elect as the next president and if that person will be supportive of the transgender community, of people like her.

Her father, Craig, worries about that, too.

“You want your child to have an easy life, for everybody to love your child. And with the flip of a switch, your child is going to have one of the most difficult life scenarios possible.”





Kimma’s birth / Andy Robinson

WHAT’S IN A NAME?

Sitting in the hospital room post-delivery holding their son, Craig and his wife Jodi still didn’t know what to call their first born.

To the outsider, coming to the conclusion of naming their baby boy after his father may seem like an obvious choice, but Craig certainly couldn’t see it — mostly because he had never really liked his own name.

“I never thought my name was anything special or terrible so… but I thought well maybe if we name him Craig, I’ll like my name more. In fact, I’ll certainly like my name more.”

But Craig’s son didn’t like the name the name he shared with his father. He hated it.

“That was tough because I was feeling like, is there something wrong with me as a person that my son isn’t proud to be associated with me,” an injury that grew larger as his son got older and more uncomfortable in his own body.

According to the American Psychiatric Association, gender dysphoria is, “a conflict between a person’s physical or assigned gender and the gender with which he/she/they identify.” Some children may not express discomfort until puberty or after, while others express feelings and behaviors relating to gender dysphoria at 4 years old or younger.

Craig’s son was the latter.





Craig recounts Kimma’s transition from his son as Craig Jr. to his daugher. From left to right: Craig, Teresa, Kimma, Jodi. / Andy Robinson

GROWING PAINS

Craig noticed that his son never picked any of the male characters as his avatar. He always elected to be Princess Peach in the Wii console game, Mario Kart, his first conscious gender expression.

“It was just constant” Craig remembers,”We’d be over [at] someone’s house, and [he] would insist on being the girl character, and I’m thinking silently to myself ‘Just be the boy character.’ But [he] had no problem saying, ‘This is what I’m gonna be.’ So that was the beginning of us noticing there’s some draw here toward the feminine.”

A few months later, during a family gathering involving home videos and old baby pictures, Craig, Jr., got visibly upset, but neither Craig nor his wife knew why.

“We didn’t know how it was affecting [him],” Craig said.

“And then for a while we didn’t really look at those pictures at all.”

The final confirmation that his son didn’t feel right in his own skin came when he was only six years old.

Sobbing in his arms, Craig struggled to hold onto his son — both literally and metaphorically, “The pain was so strong that I could feel in my body from holding you,” Craig said while looking at Kimma. “That energy just wanting to fly out, it was really intense. I knew I was dealing with something that was very powerful and out of my pay range.”

Six years later, Craig’s only son began transitioning into his eldest daughter, Kimma.

TRANSGENDER IN TRUMP’S AMERICA

Craig’s struggle to adapt to his new family reflects the current political landscape in the U.S. Last year, North Carolina became the first state to restrict which restrooms transgender individuals can use. Though the bill was repealed in March, 16 other states have followed suit, some even include policy proposals specific to public schools, rather than all public facilities, according to the National Conference of State Legislatures.

In July, President Trump tweeted that individuals who identify as transgender are compromising the military, costing the Defense department money, and would not be permitted to serve their country moving forward.

“Visibility makes a difference,” Perotti says, “Whenever you talk about a discrimination or marginalization that’s steeped in secrecy and silence, anything that increases visibility makes a difference.”

Kimma wholeheartedly agrees, ”Once you get rid of the pressures of society, the pressures of family, and you just listen to yourself, you can figure out who you are -- obviously [things] have become better for me, I have more confidence. I’m not fake, like a billboard [saying], “Oh, this is Craig, but what you don’t know is there’s a crystal behind [him] named Kimma.”

Though Trump’s statements are not supported by the Department of Defense, existing law, or any proposed legislation, it’s concerning for transgender people currently serving in the military.

Craig understands how fortunate they are to live in Massachusetts, a state with strong laws and policies that ensure the rights of all students to be able to attend school free of discrimination or harassment.

“When you make your school safe for those who are the most vulnerable it really makes it a safe environment for all students,” Perotti adds.

Though it’s comforting to know Kimma is safe at school, it doesn’t strip Craig of his parental fear -- fear that his daughter may be confronted by transphobia and harmed for something that she struggled to discover and fighting to preserve.

Like America, Craig’s progression to Kimma’s transition is gradual. “It’s a process. There’s some days I’m celebratory, sometimes I go back down a few notches and I’m not celebrating it. But I’m [not] rejecting it anymore and I don’t think I’ll ever go back to that.”
